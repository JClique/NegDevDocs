# Equipment and materials

Darkroom printing requires a few essential tools.
Beyond the basics, additional equipment can make the process easier, more consistent, or more enjoyable.

## Required tools

### Enlarger & timer

An [enlarger](/glossary#enlarger) projects light through a [negative](/glossary#negative) onto [photographic paper](/glossary#photographic-paper) to create a print.
Different models are designed to handle different negative sizes, offering flexibility depending on the format you're working with.

A timer connects to the enlarger to control the duration of exposure.
Accurate timing is crucial for producing consistent results and is an essential part of the darkroom workflow.

### Darkroom printing paper

The choice of darkroom printing paper significantly affects the final print quality and the overall look and feel of your image.

Paper is typically available in two main types: [resin‑coated (RC)](/glossary#resin-coated-rc-paper) and [fibre‑based (FB)](/glossary#fibre-based-fb-paper).
RC papers are easier and faster to process and dry, making them ideal for quick results and everyday printing.
FB papers offer greater depth and richness in tone but require longer washing and drying times.

Both types come in a variety of surfaces, including glossy, matte, and satin, each providing different aesthetic results and textures.

### Developing trays

Developing trays are essential for holding the chemicals used in the printing process.
They come in various sizes and materials, allowing for the efficient processing of multiple prints simultaneously while ensuring the chemicals are contained safely.

## Helpful tools

These tools are not essential but make darkroom printing **easier and more efficient**.

### Easel

The [easel](/glossary#easel) holds the paper flat during exposure.
Different types are available and contribute to precise alignment and clean borders.

### Print tongs

[Print tongs](/glossary#print-tongs) let you move prints between chemical trays without touching the emulsion.
They help avoid damage or fingerprints and keep chemicals from cross‑contaminating.

### Focus finder

A [grain focuser](/glossary#grain-focuser) (focus finder) helps achieve critical focus by magnifying the projected grain on the baseboard.

### Timer/clock

A timer or clock is useful during chemical processing as it helps control tray times.
Consistent timing promotes repeatable results.

### Drying rack

Drying is essential for preserving print quality. Options include air drying and specialised dryers.
Proper drying prevents damage and ensures longevity.

### Dodging/burning tools

[Dodging and burning](/glossary#dodging-and-burning) tools selectively alter exposure in specific areas of the print.
They are critical for fine‑tuning contrast and highlights.

### Exposure printing scale

An exposure printing scale helps you find the right exposure time for your darkroom print.
It works by placing a step wedge—a strip with areas of varying density—under the enlarger light and exposing it onto photo paper.
The resulting print shows how different exposure times affect the paper.
By examining this print, you can quickly estimate the best exposure time for your negative.
This tool reduces paper waste during testing and keeps results consistent across sessions.

## Luxury tools

For those who print frequently, these tools provide extra convenience and precision.

### Print dryer

A print dryer expedites drying while helping keep dust off the surface for quicker, cleaner results.

### Print processor

A print processor automates tray development, improving efficiency and consistency for high‑volume printing.
It is ideal for [RA‑4](/glossary#ra-4), where the chemistry does not lend itself to open‑tray developing.
